# Masterclass Guide to UX Design

## Role Framing

Excellence in UX design means relentlessly understanding real human needs and shaping products so people can accomplish their goals with clarity, ease, and dignity. It demands equal fluency in research, systems thinking, craft, and business judgment — UX is not decoration, it's the design of how things work.

## Best-in-Class Voices

- **Don Norman** — Coined "user experience"; author of _The Design of Everyday Things_. Emphasizes affordances, signifiers, feedback, and human-centered design rooted in cognitive science.
- **Jakob Nielsen** — Co-founder of NN/g; pioneer of usability engineering. Known for the 10 Usability Heuristics, discount usability testing, and "Jakob's Law" (users spend most of their time on other sites).
- **Steve Krug** — Author of _Don't Make Me Think_. Champions self-evident design, plain language, and cheap, frequent usability testing over elaborate process.
- **Erika Hall** — Author of _Just Enough Research_ and _Conversational Design_. Pushes designers to treat research as a core skill and to question assumptions before pixels.
- **Julie Zhuo** — Former VP of Design at Facebook, author of _The Making of a Manager_. Strong on design judgment, critique culture, and shipping in real organizations.
- **Alan Cooper** — Father of "personas" and Goal-Directed Design; author of _About Face_ and _The Inmates Are Running the Asylum_. Focuses on interaction design fundamentals and designing for goals, not features.

## Core Idea

Great UX is the disciplined translation of real user goals into the simplest possible interaction that respects people's time, attention, and intelligence — and the only way to know if you've done it is to watch real people try.

## Essential Best Practices

1. **Start with the problem, not the solution.**
   _Why it matters:_ Most product failures are problems poorly understood, not solutions poorly executed. Jumping to wireframes hides flawed assumptions.
   _How to apply:_ Before designing, articulate who the user is, what job they're trying to get done, in what context, and why current options fail. Write it in one paragraph a stakeholder can challenge.

2. **Do just enough research — but actually do it.**
   _Why it matters:_ Designers consistently overestimate how well they understand users. Even small qualitative studies kill bad ideas cheaply.
   _How to apply:_ Talk to 5–8 real users per question. Use open-ended interviews, contextual observation, and usability tests on rough prototypes. Favor speed and frequency over polish.

3. **Design for goals and tasks, not features.**
   _Why it matters:_ Feature lists grow; user goals stay stable. Goal-driven design produces coherent products instead of cluttered ones.
   _How to apply:_ For every screen, ask: "What is the user trying to accomplish here, and what's the shortest path?" Cut anything that doesn't serve that path.

4. **Make the interface self-evident.**
   _Why it matters:_ Users don't read, they scan; they don't study, they muddle through. Cognitive load is the enemy of completion.
   _How to apply:_ Apply Nielsen's heuristics — visibility of system status, match to real-world language, consistency, error prevention, recognition over recall. If a new user needs an explanation, redesign instead of adding a tooltip.

5. **Prototype early, test cheaply, iterate fast.**
   _Why it matters:_ No amount of internal debate equals five minutes of watching a real user struggle. Fidelity should match the question you're answering.
   _How to apply:_ Sketch first, click-through prototype second, polished mock last. Test with 5 users per round (per Nielsen, this catches ~85% of usability issues), fix the top problems, retest.

6. **Respect the entire user journey, not just the screen.**
   _Why it matters:_ UX includes onboarding, edge cases, errors, empty states, recovery, and what happens after the "happy path." These are where trust is built or destroyed.
   _How to apply:_ Map the end-to-end journey including failure modes. Design empty states, error messages, and loading states with the same care as the primary flow.

7. **Write the words first.**
   _Why it matters:_ Most UX problems are language problems. Clear copy often eliminates the need for clever interaction design.
   _How to apply:_ Draft button labels, headings, and error messages in plain language before laying out the screen. If you can't explain it in a sentence, the design isn't ready.

8. **Earn your seat by speaking the business's language.**
   _Why it matters:_ Designers who can only argue from taste get overruled. Designers who connect design decisions to user outcomes and business metrics get trusted with bigger decisions.
   _How to apply:_ Frame proposals around the problem, the evidence, the trade-offs, and the expected outcome. Measure post-launch and share what you learn.

## Common Mistakes

- Designing from personal preference or internal opinion instead of user evidence.
- Confusing aesthetic polish with usability — a beautiful screen users can't navigate is a failed screen.
- Treating research, accessibility, and content as someone else's job rather than core craft.

## Quick Start

- Pick one current project and write a one-paragraph problem statement: who, what goal, what context, what's broken today.
- Schedule five 30-minute user conversations this week — no slides, just open questions and listening.
- Run a usability test on your existing product with 5 users and a simple task list; note the top 3 friction points.
- Rewrite every button, heading, and error message on one key screen in plainer language.
- Read _Don't Make Me Think_ (Krug) and bookmark Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics as a recurring design checklist.
