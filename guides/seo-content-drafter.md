# Masterclass Guide to Content SEO Drafting

## Role Framing

A Content SEO Drafter executes a finalized brief into a publication-ready draft — translating an outline, target keyword, sources, and internal-link plan into prose that ranks, reads well, and ships without rework. Excellence here is craft and discipline: faithful execution of the brief, search-intent fluency, and the ability to write scannable, source-backed copy fast.

## Core Idea

Great SEO drafting is faithful execution: deliver the brief's outline, keyword, sources, and links exactly as specified, while writing prose clear and useful enough that a human reader stays to the end. The draft must satisfy intent in the first scroll and earn the rest of the page.

## Essential Best Practices

**1. Treat the brief as a contract, not a starting point.**
Why it matters: The brief reflects upstream decisions — SERP analysis, keyword strategy, link equity planning — that the drafter is explicitly out of scope to revisit. Drifting from it creates rework and breaks the strategy.
How to apply: Before writing, checklist every required element — H1, H2s in order, target keyword, secondary terms, internal links with exact anchor text, source citations, metadata fields, author bio. Draft against the checklist; do not "improve" the outline mid-draft. If something seems wrong, flag it as a comment, but ship the version specified.

**2. Deliver on the title within the first 100 words.**
Why it matters: Search intent is judged in seconds. Long preambles invite pogo-sticking back to the SERP, and "delay the answer" intros are the single most common reason briefs come back for revision.
How to apply: Open with a sentence that explicitly answers or frames the title's promise. State who the piece is for and what they'll get. Save context, history, and personal anecdotes for later sections — or cut them entirely if the brief doesn't call for them.

**3. Write for scanners first, readers second.**
Why it matters: Most search visitors scan before they commit. Wall-of-text drafts underperform regardless of content quality, and most briefs explicitly require scannable formatting.
How to apply: Keep paragraphs to 1–3 sentences. Maintain a clear H2 cadence — a new subhead roughly every 200–300 words. Use bullets and numbered lists where the content is genuinely list-shaped, not as decoration. Front-load each section: the answer in the first sentence, support after.

**4. Place the target keyword naturally and only where it belongs.**
Why it matters: Modern ranking rewards topical coverage and intent match, not density. Forced repetition reads as spam to both readers and quality systems.
How to apply: Use the exact target keyword in the H1, the first 100 words, at least one H2 where it fits naturally, and the metadata. Beyond that, use natural variations and related entities. If a sentence reads worse with the keyword in it, rewrite the sentence — don't keep the awkward keyword.

**5. Honor source citations and internal links exactly as specified.**
Why it matters: Citations and internal links are strategic assets — they support claims, distribute authority, and signal expertise. The brief has decided which sources to cite and which pages to link with which anchor text; the drafter's job is precise placement.
How to apply: Cite each specified source at the claim it actually supports, not as a decorative footnote. Insert internal links at the specified locations with the exact anchor text given — no paraphrasing the anchor, no relocating to a "better" spot. If a specified source seems weak, that is out of scope to relitigate; place it as instructed.

**6. Make claims concrete and attributable.**
Why it matters: Vague, unsourced assertions are the signature of low-quality content and the fastest way to fail an editorial review. Specificity is also what makes prose actually useful.
How to apply: Replace hedge phrases ("many experts believe," "studies show") with the specific source the brief provided. Prefer numbers, named people, and dated examples to abstractions. If the brief doesn't provide a source for a claim, soften the claim rather than invent support.

**7. Write the metadata and bio as carefully as the body.**
Why it matters: The title tag and meta description are the ad for the page; the author bio is a primary E-E-A-T signal. Both are frequently rushed and frequently the reason a draft underperforms.
How to apply: Title tag — fit within roughly 50–60 characters, lead with the target keyword, signal value. Meta description — roughly 140–160 characters, deliver on the title and include a reason to click. Author bio — credentials relevant to the topic, in the format and length the brief specifies.

**8. Self-edit for fluff before you submit.**
Why it matters: Drafts get rejected for padding more than for any other prose-level reason — and padding is what separates a "Helpful Content"-style draft from one that smells AI-generated.
How to apply: After the first pass, read the draft once with a single question: _does this sentence earn its place?_ Cut throat-clearing intros to sections, restated points, and "in today's fast-paced world" openers. If you can delete a sentence with no loss of meaning, delete it.

## Common Mistakes

- **Editing the outline mid-draft** — adding or merging H2s, changing the angle, or "improving" the keyword. This is explicitly out of scope and almost always triggers a redo.
- **Front-loading throat-clearing instead of the answer** — burying the deliverable behind history, definitions, or personal framing the brief never asked for.
- **Mistreating links and sources as decoration** — citing a source far from the claim it supports, or rewriting the specified anchor text because it "reads better."

## Quick Start

- Build a one-page checklist from the brief — H1, H2 order, target keyword, secondary terms, internal links + exact anchors, required sources, metadata, author bio — and tick each item as you draft.
- Write the intro last, but constrain yourself: 75–100 words that deliver on the title and name the reader.
- Set a paragraph cap (3 sentences) and an H2 cadence (every 200–300 words) before you start; enforce both on the editing pass.
- Place every specified internal link and source citation on the first pass, at the exact location and anchor specified — don't leave them for cleanup.
- Do a dedicated fluff pass: read top to bottom and cut any sentence that doesn't add information, an example, or a transition the reader needs.
