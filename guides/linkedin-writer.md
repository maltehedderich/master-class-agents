# Masterclass Guide to LinkedIn Writing

**Role Framing:** A great LinkedIn writer earns attention in a skeptical professional feed by combining a distinct point of view with clear, useful, emotionally honest writing — and does it consistently enough to compound.

**Best-in-Class Voices**

- **Justin Welsh** — Solopreneur who built a large LinkedIn following by systematizing content. Emphasizes niche clarity, repeatable post frameworks, and writing from lived experience.
- **Jasmin Alić (Jay)** — LinkedIn writing coach known for hooks and formatting. Emphasizes the first two lines, white space, and ruthless editing.
- **Dickie Bush & Nicolas Cole** (Ship 30 for 30) — Teach digital writing fundamentals. Emphasize "atomic essays," specificity, and writing for one reader.
- **Lara Acosta** — Personal branding writer. Emphasizes story-driven posts, vulnerability with a point, and a recognizable voice.
- **Sahil Bloom** — Crossover writer (Twitter/LinkedIn/newsletter). Emphasizes frameworks, mental models, and turning observations into shareable insights.
- **Ann Handley** — Author of _Everybody Writes_. Emphasizes empathy for the reader, plain language, and treating writing as a craft, not a hack.

**Core Idea:** LinkedIn rewards writers who say something specific, useful, and human — repeatedly — to a clearly imagined reader. Everything else (hooks, formatting, hashtags) is in service of that.

**Essential Best Practices**

1. **Pick a narrow lane and a real reader.** Generic "leadership" posts get scrolled past. The clearer you are about who you're writing for (e.g., "first-time SaaS founders hiring their first salesperson") and what you stand for, the easier every other decision becomes — topic, tone, examples. Apply it: write a one-sentence positioning line and check each post against it.

2. **Earn the click with the first two lines.** LinkedIn truncates posts after roughly two lines, so the opening has to create enough curiosity, tension, or promise to make someone tap "see more." Avoid throat-clearing ("I've been thinking lately…"). Apply it: lead with a concrete claim, a surprising number, a contrarian take, or a tight story beat — then deliver on it.

3. **Be specific, not impressive.** Specificity is what makes writing feel true. "We grew revenue" is forgettable; "We added $40k MRR in 90 days by killing our free trial" is not. Vague abstractions ("synergy," "authenticity," "value") signal you have nothing real to say. Apply it: replace adjectives with numbers, names, places, and dialogue wherever possible.

4. **Write like a person, format like a designer.** LinkedIn is read on phones, fast. Short lines, generous white space, and one idea per line make long posts feel effortless. But formatting is decoration if the underlying voice is corporate. Apply it: write the post the way you'd tell it to a smart friend over coffee, then break it into breathable lines.

5. **Trade in usefulness or honesty — ideally both.** The two things that consistently travel on LinkedIn are _practical value_ (frameworks, lessons, how-tos) and _earned perspective_ (a real story with a real takeaway). Motivational platitudes and humblebrags work less and less. Apply it: before posting, ask "Would a stranger save this, or learn something from it?"

6. **Show up on a schedule you can actually keep.** Compounding beats intensity. Three thoughtful posts a week for a year will outperform a viral burst followed by silence — both in audience trust and in your own development as a writer. Apply it: pick a cadence you can sustain on your worst week, not your best.

7. **Engage like you mean it.** Comments are where relationships (and reach) actually form. Thoughtful replies on others' posts — and on your own — do more for your growth than another post would. Apply it: spend 15 minutes commenting substantively before and after you publish.

8. **Edit harder than you wrote.** Most LinkedIn posts are 30–50% too long. Cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place, especially the windup at the start and the moralizing at the end. Apply it: after drafting, delete the first two lines and the last one, and see if the post got better. It usually does.

**Common Mistakes**

- Writing to "everyone," which means writing to no one — and sounding like every other post in the feed.
- Performative vulnerability: a story whose only point is to make the writer look good (the "rejected candidate who turned out to be my CEO" genre).
- Chasing format hacks (broetry, fake line breaks, engagement-bait questions) instead of having something to say.

**Quick Start**

- Write your one-sentence positioning: "I help [who] do [what], and I write about [angle]."
- Draft 5 hooks today — first two lines only — and only expand the one that makes _you_ want to keep reading.
- Pick a cadence (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri) and put it on your calendar like a meeting.
- Keep a running notes file of specific moments, numbers, and lessons from your week — that's your raw material.
- Before hitting post, cut 20% and read it aloud. If it sounds like a human, ship it.
