# Masterclass Guide to Marketing Writing for Landing Pages & Conversion Copy

**Role Framing:** A landing page writer's job isn't to write — it's to help a specific reader make a decision. Excellence requires equal parts research (knowing the reader's exact words and objections), persuasion architecture (sequencing belief), and ruthless editing for clarity over cleverness.

**Best-in-Class Voices**

- **Eugene Schwartz** — _Breakthrough Advertising_ (1966). The most cited direct-response book ever written. His **Stages of Awareness** and **Stages of Sophistication** frameworks remain the foundation for matching message to reader.
- **David Ogilvy** — _Ogilvy on Advertising_, _Confessions of an Advertising Man_. The reference for headline craft, research-led writing, and the discipline of treating the reader as intelligent. Famous for the claim that the headline does ~80% of the work.
- **Claude Hopkins** — _Scientific Advertising_ (1923). The originator of testable, specific, claim-based copy. His insistence that "specific facts beat generalities" is still the single most violated rule in marketing.
- **Joanna Wiebe** — Founder of Copyhackers; effectively coined "conversion copywriting." Best modern voice on **Voice-of-Customer research**, message mining from reviews, and the message-hierarchy of a landing page.
- **Harry Dry** — _Marketing Examples_. The clearest contemporary teacher of concrete, specific, "show-don't-tell" copy in short form. Excellent for headline and hero-section craft.
- **Gary Halbert** — _The Boron Letters_. Old-school direct response, but unmatched on the centrality of the **offer** and the discipline of writing to one person.

**Core Idea:** Great landing-page copy isn't written — it's _assembled_ from the reader's own language, organized around their stage of awareness, and pressure-tested against a single decision. Persuasion is research plus sequencing; style is what's left after you remove everything that didn't earn its place.

**Essential Best Practices**

1. **Mine the voice of the customer before you write a word.** Readers convert on copy that sounds like their own internal monologue. Writing from imagination almost always produces vague claims that the writer believes and the reader doesn't. Apply it: pull 50–100 verbatim phrases from reviews, sales calls, support tickets, Reddit threads, and cancellation surveys. Tag them by pain, desired outcome, objection, and "aha" moment. Your headline and subhead should be 70%+ paraphrased from this corpus, not invented.

2. **Match copy to the reader's stage of awareness (Schwartz).** The same product needs radically different copy for someone who has never heard of the problem vs. someone comparing you to a named competitor. Mismatch is the #1 silent killer of landing pages. Apply it: decide which of the five levels the page targets — Unaware, Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware, Product-Aware, Most Aware — _before_ drafting. Unaware traffic needs story and problem agitation; Most Aware traffic needs offer, proof, and friction removal. One page, one stage.

3. **Earn the click on the headline; earn the scroll on the subhead.** If the hero section doesn't pass the 5-second test ("What is this, who is it for, why should I care?"), nothing below it gets read. Apply it: write 20–30 headline variants before choosing. Force each to answer one of: a specific outcome, a specific reader, a specific mechanism, or a specific contrast. Cut every word the page would still work without. Pair the headline with a subhead that adds a _new_ piece of information, not a restatement.

4. **Be specific — numbers, names, mechanisms, time.** Hopkins' oldest finding still holds: vague superlatives ("best-in-class," "revolutionary," "seamless") are read as noise, while specifics are read as evidence. Specificity is the cheapest form of credibility. Apply it: replace every adjective with a fact. "Fast" → "Loads in 1.2s." "Trusted by professionals" → "Used by 412 ICU nurses at Mount Sinai." If you can't quantify, name. If you can't name, describe a mechanism.

5. **Sell outcomes and identity, not features.** Readers buy a better version of their life, not your product. Features are inputs to that promise — they belong in the proof section, not the hero. Apply it: for every feature, force a "so what?" until you reach an emotional or status outcome. Lead the page with the outcome; use features as evidence the outcome is real. The structure is: _promise → proof → mechanism → offer_, not the reverse.

6. **Engineer one decision and remove every other path.** A landing page is not a website. Multiple CTAs, navigation menus, and "alternatives" dilute the single conversion you're trying to earn. Apply it: define the one action before writing. Strip nav. Repeat one CTA every ~1.5 screens with the same verb. Anticipate the top 3–5 objections in order (price, trust, fit, effort, risk) and answer each in the page's flow rather than leaving them to fester.

7. **Write to one person, out loud.** Copy that reads like a brochure was written to a committee. Copy that converts sounds like a smart friend explaining something at a kitchen table. Apply it: pick a real customer (or composite) and write the page as a Loom script to them. Use "you," contractions, and short sentences. Read every paragraph aloud; if you stumble, the reader will too. Cut adverbs and qualifiers ruthlessly.

8. **Treat the page as a hypothesis, not a deliverable.** Even great copywriters guess wrong. The discipline of the field — going back to Hopkins — is that the market is the only honest critic. Apply it: ship a clear v1 fast. Instrument scroll depth, CTA clicks, and form starts. Test one variable at a time (usually the hero or offer first — they have the highest leverage). Keep a swipe file of what won and _why you think_ it won; pattern recognition compounds over years.

**Common Mistakes**

- Writing in "we" voice and feature lists when readers convert on outcome-led, "you"-voice copy grounded in their own words.
- Skipping awareness-level diagnosis, so the page either over-explains to ready buyers or under-sells to cold traffic.
- Burying the offer behind hedging, jargon, or politeness — readers can't accept a deal they can't quickly see, price, and understand the risk of.

**Quick Start**

- Spend the first 60% of project time on research, not writing. Pull 50+ verbatim customer phrases and tag them by pain/outcome/objection before drafting.
- Decide the one stage of awareness and the one action the page targets. Write both at the top of your draft doc.
- Write 20 headlines. Pick the most specific one — the one with a number, a name, or a mechanism in it — not the cleverest.
- Apply the "so what?" test to every line. If the answer isn't a reader outcome, cut it or move it to a proof block.
- Read the final draft aloud, then cut 20%. Whatever survives is closer to copy that converts.
