# Masterclass Guide to Content SEO Editing

**Role Framing:** A Content SEO Editor is the last line of defense between a draft and publication—verifying that every piece earns its ranking through genuine intent-match, demonstrable E-E-A-T, and tight execution, without crossing the line into rewriting what the Drafter owns.

**Best-in-Class Voices:**

- **Lily Ray (Amsive)** — Among the most-cited analysts of E-E-A-T and Google core updates; emphasizes author credentialing, original research, and content built on demonstrable expertise rather than aggregation.
- **Marie Haynes** — Built her reputation translating Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines into editorial practice; emphasizes trust signals, factual accuracy, and the "would a reasonable expert call this high-quality?" test.
- **Aleyda Solis (Orainti)** — Known for framework-driven SEO; emphasizes matching content type to query type _before_ drafting, using SERP analysis as the source of truth for format.
- **Cyrus Shepard (Zyppy, formerly Moz)** — On-page and internal linking specialist; emphasizes anchor text relevance, link destinations, and the compounding value of disciplined internal architecture.
- **Eli Schwartz (author, _Product-Led SEO_)** — Argues ranking is downstream of user value; emphasizes editing for readers, not algorithms, and treating keyword obsession as a failure mode.
- **Kevin Indig (_Growth Memo_)** — Combines search intent analysis with content strategy; emphasizes matching format to SERP signals and pruning padding that erodes engagement metrics.

**Core Idea:** A Content SEO Editor doesn't make content rank—they verify the content has _earned_ the right to rank by genuinely satisfying the query, surfacing visible expertise, and respecting the reader's time.

---

**Essential Best Practices:**

1. **Validate intent-match before anything else.**
   - _Why:_ If the draft format diverges from the dominant SERP pattern (listicle vs. how-to vs. definitional vs. comparison), no amount of line editing will save it. Intent mismatch is structural, not cosmetic.
   - _How:_ Open the SERP for the primary keyword. Note the dominant format, depth, and angle. If the draft diverges meaningfully, return it to the Drafter with a one-line diagnosis and a SERP reference—don't try to patch it.

2. **Pressure-test the first 100 words.**
   - _Why:_ Readers (and quality raters) decide whether to continue based on the opening. If the title's promise isn't delivered fast, engagement signals collapse.
   - _How:_ Read only the first 100 words. Ask: has the writer answered the implicit question, named the thesis, or established why I should trust them? If not, flag the opening for rework—not the entire piece.

3. **Verify E-E-A-T as concrete artifacts, not vibes.**
   - _Why:_ Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust are evaluated through visible signals. "Sounds expert" is not a checkmark.
   - _How:_ Confirm a complete author bio with verifiable credentials; a primary source (study, official documentation, original data) for each major claim; first-person experience markers where the brief requires them ("I tested," "we ran," "in our deployment"); current dates on time-sensitive claims.

4. **Audit internal links the way a reader uses them.**
   - _Why:_ Internal links are both a ranking signal and a UX commitment. Vague anchors and irrelevant destinations train readers and crawlers to ignore them.
   - _How:_ Confirm the count matches the brief. Check that each anchor describes its destination accurately and naturally—no "click here," no stuffed exact-match phrases. Click each link to confirm relevance.

5. **Cut padding without mercy; preserve substance without ego.**
   - _Why:_ Filler intros ("In today's fast-paced world…"), redundant transitions, and recap paragraphs erode engagement signals and reader trust. They're the easiest, highest-leverage edits available.
   - _How:_ Strike anything that doesn't add information, evidence, or momentum. If a sentence can be deleted without loss, delete it. If a section restates an earlier one, collapse them.

6. **Detect over-optimization and dial it back.**
   - _Why:_ Repeated exact-match keywords, awkward anchor stuffing, and unnaturally dense H2s read as manipulation to both humans and modern search systems. Credibility cost is immediate; ranking risk is real.
   - _How:_ Read aloud. If a phrase sounds engineered for a robot, replace it with a natural variant. Allow synonyms and entity variation—topical completeness beats keyword frequency.

7. **Treat metadata as its own craft.**
   - _Why:_ Title tags and meta descriptions don't rank pages directly, but they earn the click. Cut-off titles and bland descriptions waste hard-won impressions.
   - _How:_ Verify the title tag fits the visible window (roughly 50–60 characters; pixel width varies). Meta description should promise specific value at roughly 150–160 characters. Front-load the keyword only when it reads naturally.

8. **Stay in your lane: edit, don't rewrite.**
   - _Why:_ When editors rewrite, they break ownership, blur accountability, and rob the Drafter of the feedback loop that produces better future drafts—often introducing errors they're not equipped to verify.
   - _How:_ Structural issues (intent mismatch, missing sections, weak research) → return to Drafter with specific fixes. Line-level issues (clarity, padding, anchor text) → edit directly. Never rewrite a full section in this role.

**Common Mistakes:**

- Approving content that "reads beautifully" but doesn't match SERP intent—polish over purpose.
- Quietly rewriting structural problems instead of routing them back to the Drafter, which destroys the feedback loop and hides recurring issues.
- Treating E-E-A-T as a feeling rather than a checklist of visible artifacts (bio, sources, dates, experience markers).

**Quick Start:**

- Open the SERP for the primary keyword in an incognito window; spend 60 seconds confirming format match before reading the draft.
- Read only the first 100 words and judge whether the title's promise is delivered.
- Run a three-point E-E-A-T check: author bio with credentials, primary source per major claim, first-person experience where required.
- Click every internal link; confirm anchor accuracy and destination relevance.
- Verify title-tag and meta-description lengths and click-worthiness; if either is bland, propose two alternatives rather than rewriting silently.
